The handle can rotate a max of 90 degrees down towards the user, at which point the water will be flowing at max rate. The main force is in the plane of the crank rotation. The part where it attaches to the tap mechanism in the faucet has a square socket to transmit torque. Due to the small amount of force needed to transmit torque, the corners of the square socket whow little signs of wear and the crank seems very sturdy.

Simplify and Analyze

To start thinking about how strong a crank needs to be, we need to get a sense of the forces it might experience. Otherwise it’s hard to know what materials would be appropriate to use or how thick it should be. At design time, this kind of early check can prevent us from designing things that are much too strong or too weak. Later (Part 2) we’ll look at loads in more detail from a CAD model.

  1. Coordinate frames

To talk a bit more precisely about forces, we need a diagram. To be consistent, let us set up two (CAD style) x,y,z coordinate frames. We will embed the first frame  (xo, yo, zo) at the center of the rotating shaft, with zo pointing outward along the shaft axis and with xo pointing along the crank and yo pointing upward when the crank is in the three o’clock position (see Fig. 2 for an example).  Embed a second coordinate frame (xh,yh,zh) at the approximate location of the applied force at the handle. Align this coordinate frame so that it is parallel with (xo, yo, zo). Now draw a vector r = [rx,ry,rz] from (xo, yo, zo) to  (xh,yh,zh). What are the components rx, ry, rz in millimeters? Draw both front (xy plane) and top (xz plane) views of the crank and coordinate frames, and draw the corresponding force vectors, Fy and Fz where they belong.

  1. Torque or moment

Recall that torque or moment is the product of force x lever arm. So if you yank with a force of 10N at the end of a wrench that is 0.1 meters long, you generate 1.0Nm of torque on a bolt. Given this relationship, what is the maximum cranking moment or torque, Mz = Fx*ry? And what is the perpendicular bending moment My = Fz*rx? Fig 6. Simulation diagram on CAD

First, I set the material of the shaft to stainless steel and adjusted the material of the crank to match PLA plastic. I fixed one side of the crank, and applied a 10N downward force to the shaft on the other side. I left the mesh setting at default, and then I ran the FEA simulation.

        Overall, the maximum displacement in the y direction was about 2.43 mm. I can assume that the curved monkey-tail handle might have contributed to more displacement, since it added weight at the end of the crank.

The crank had an average experimental vertical deflection of about 0.110in.

In our simulation, the vertical deflection was about 2.43mm, which converts to about 0.095in. When calculating the percent error, this is about (.110-.095)/.110 = 13.6% error, which is quite close. Sources of error may be due to imperfect testing conditions which are not reflected in the virtual simulation. For example, due to the process of 3D printing of the part, the estimate of Young’s modulus for the PLA plastic used in the simulation may be greater than the actual Young’s modulus of, and therefore lead to a smaller experimental deflection. In addition, if the bolts at the connection of the base are not perfectly rigid, there may be more rotation in the crank, leading to more vertical displacement. We did not notice the dial gage slipping, so this is not likely to be a source of error.

  1. Reflection

This design was not on the heavier side, but still very sturdy. Since the crank only deflected .1in, it is likely that the crank would be able to withstand 2x higher load while staying within the same bounding box. However, a few changes could be made to reduce deflection. For one, increasing the infill ratio, although making the part more costly and bulky, would increase Young’s Modulus of the material, and therefore reduce the deflection. In addition, the simulation showed that most of the stress was at the end of the crank, so thickening the end of the crank may increase support, and therefore reduce deflection. Overall, this project was an educational experience in CAD, design, modeling simulations, and testing in real life.
